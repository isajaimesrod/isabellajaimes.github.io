Research on technology, labour, and Latinx migrant worlds.

I study how Latina migrant housecleaners in Toronto navigate agencies, platforms, and informal networks that shape how their labour is valued and controlled. I work with cleaners to map their daily struggles and co-design alternative infrastructures of care. I ask: what would technologies look like if they were built with the workers who sustain our cities?

About me

research · storytelling · design

I am an STS and CSCW researcher whose work focuses on how technology shapes and mediates relations within immigrant Latinx communities. My research sits at the intersection of technology, labour, and social justice, with a particular interest in domestic and care work, informal economies, and platformed forms of expertise.

I am part of the Socio-technical Resistance and Ethical Technologies (STREET) Lab at the University of Toronto, where I collaborate on participatory and design-based projects with migrant workers and community organizations.

Current Work

Latina house-cleaners in Toronto

1 · Everyday scenes

Manuela*, precise and meticulous, brings her passion for organizing into every home, treating cleaning as a practice of accountability and dignity. Silvia* dreams of leaving her agency to build her own client network, imagining herself not just as a worker but as an entrepreneur carving her autonomy in a new country. Teresa*, balancing clients who pay poorly and agencies that demand too much, stays in the job because it remains the most viable path toward stability despite its contradictions.

2 · Shared struggles

Their aspirations differ—entrepreneurship, regularity, mobility—but they converge in the material struggle of making a life in Canada while navigating language barriers, unclear legal conditions, and the compromises required for survival. It is precisely in this complexity that design becomes meaningful: as a collaborative way of sensing, mapping, and reorganizing the fragile infrastructures through which these women sustain themselves and their communities.

3 · Designing with

This project investigates and co-designs technologies that reimagine domestic work across informal contexts. Its aim is twofold: first, to make visible the macro-networks—economic flows, governance structures, technological infrastructures—that shape the value of labor and care; and second, to surface the micro-networks of community, resistance, and solidarities grounded in the lived experiences of migrant women. By working across these scales, the project explores how technological design can support building pluriversal futures of care and work.

Present projects

Current · CSCW paper

El costo de la independencia

Through 19 interviews with Latina house-cleaners in Toronto during 2022, we found how identity shapes experiences and strategies in the labour markets. We find that Latino house-cleaners prefer to use asynchronous digital marketplaces, such as social media commerce groups and online classified advertisement websites, as the platforms provides flexibility and control over their labour outcomes.

Collaborative · CHI 2024

Networks of care in digital domestic labour economies

In collaboration with STREET Lab, this project examines how domestic workers build networks of care, support and resistance within platform infrastructures. We explore how informal WhatsApp groups, shared spreadsheets and tacit knowledges of cleaning become critical infrastructures that exceed the platform.

Completed

Domestic Work Platforms in Colombia

Domestic work in Colombia has long been undervalued and largely informal. New digital platforms, like Hogaru, promise stability by directly hiring workers and offering benefits. Through interface ethnography and interviews, we examine how their matching, ranking, and pricing algorithms shape workers’ daily experiences and relationships. Our study highlights both the protections these platforms introduce and the new challenges created by algorithmic decision-making.

Completed · Bogotá

Mapping Movement

This project explores how ride-hailing platforms shape drivers' everyday geographies in Bogotá. By interviewing them, we show how drivers negotiate algorithmic control, urban inequality and the desire for mobility as a form of future-making.

  • Outputs
  • Article in preparation based on this study.

Completed · Social media

Between Compliance and Resistance

Through computational methods, we studied how social media platforms mediate conversations around fitness, health and mental health among young people in Colombia. We trace how different platforms encourage distinct forms of credibility, storytelling and gendered digital violence, and how users carve out spaces of care and refusal.

How I work

critical & collaborative methods

Critical & feminist ethnography

I approach ethnography as a practice of learning the city alongside the women who sustain it. Rather than “collecting data”, I follow everyday routes, conversations and textures of work to understand how power, care and aspiration are distributed across infrastructures.

Participatory & design-based research

Workshops, audio-stories and speculative exercises become ways of thinking together about work, value and futures. I treat design not as a solutionist tool but as a space to reorganise relations and make visible precarious infrastructures of survival.

Digital methods & mixed approaches

I combine interviews, participant observation and walkthroughs with basic scraping, mapping and network analysis, to trace how digital platforms mediate labour and visibility. Quantitative traces are always interpreted through situated, qualitative understandings.
